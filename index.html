What is Web Security?

Confidentiality

Keeping data and information private.

Accessibility

Allowing data to be accessed by the appropriate parties when called upon.

Integrity

Making sure the data remains unaltered by unauthorized parties

Who Does Web Security Effect?

In short, security effects everyone. However today we're going to focus on how it impacts developers and end users. Everyone today sends sensitive information over the internet on a regular basis, whether it's confidential work information or usernames, passwords, social security numbers, etc. Hopefully when we send this information we're protecting ourselves and being protected by the engineers who build our software.

Some Key Players

  • Developers
  • Software Architects
  • System Administrators
  • End Users

What is RSA Encryption?

History

Encryption, in short, is scrambling data in order to make it difficult to understand. Cryptography, the study of making things secret, is not only fun but extremely relevant to the modern information age.
RSA Encryption is a specific algorithm for scrambling data. RSA stands for Ron Rivest, Adi Shamir and Leonard Adleman who publicly declared the method in 1978. It is the most widely used method of encryption and underscores common practices like PGP, SSH, and SSL.

Technical

RSA Encryption is considered asymmetric encryption, which relies on each user having both a public and private key. Asymmetric encryption is considered more secure because it doesn't require the passage of sensitive information, public keys are by definition public and shareable. We'll dive more deeply into how the algorithm works in a moment.

Importance

Why does this matter? To the developer, having a deeper knowledge of the standard encryption methods allows for a more robust understanding of information security. For the end user or day to day person, having a foundational understanding of the most common encryption practice is critical to understanding how sensitive information is (or should be) handled.

A Deeper Look At The Technical

The Lock Problem

Before 1970, encryption was done symmetrically. Meaning a private key was exchanged to encrypt and decrypt messages. The question asymmetric encryption answers is, what if we don't have to share a private key? Instead, we publish a "lock". In 1978 a mathematical model was created to do just this.

One Way Functions

A full mathematical explanation is out of the scope of this lecture, however I do want to touch on the concept of one way functions. This means something that is easy to do in one direction and difficult to do in the opposite direction. For this purpose, I'm going to use a color analogy to explain the process of RSA encryption.

The Color Analogy

The color analogy shows how private information can be shared, encrypted and decrypted, without a third party learning the content of the message, and without sharing the decryption method or key.
